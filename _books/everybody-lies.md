The book discusses the role of big data and how it can be utilised to study human behaviour. The title of the book refers to the idea that humans do not always act as they speak. There is a disconnect between words and actions: data can help us bridge that gap by matching their actions to what they have said (or what we presume they think). One example is the contrast between what is said in the surveys and result of elections.

As a context, and somewhat of a side story: Google came to dominate the search market because of the hyperlink algorithm that it used. Other browsers relied on word occurance frequency on a website and that their result were not as accurate as Google’s.

The author of the book himself worked as data scientist and talks about the role of data, the big data and we can analyze it to come up with better understanding of human behaviour. One of the interesting examples is with Walmart: they analyzied their data and realized right before hurricances, pop tarts are sold at a records high. Now they were not able to understand the reason behind this choice of item but they used the insight to stock up on the items that are sold a lot.

Are news paper more left-leaning? A study by 2 Phd Stanford students who tried to assess the neutarlity of newspapers. They realized that newspapers do lean left but they left because most of the customers lean left so it’s not that there’s a conspiracy to push the leftist agenda. It’s just that most people who read the newspapers share left-related values. The newspapers adjust their leaning based on their customer so they can make money. The researchers also found out that newspapers’ politics can be identified based on the wording they use: how certain events are exagerated or down played. For instance, when gay marriage was legal, leftist would say “win for LGBQT” and right-wing would say “Marriage for same-sex marriage”.

Digital truth serum: which is when people’s words do not match their actions; say things but behave differently. Some examples:

- They don’t want to buy clothes that are made in sweatshops, but they do.
- They say that face smash was bad, and you shouldn’t stock on your freind

The question is: Are we not comfortable with the truth?

Two interesting examples: the Edmonton water management company shared their minute by minute results and during one of the famous hockey games in Canada in between the brakes all the toilets were flushing. We would hypothesize that too violent movies would increase violence in society and they did some analysis: realized that when there is a violent movie in the cinemas crime rate drops. One possible explanation: individuals who might commit crime are all in the cinema watching the movie.

The point of these two stories is that the world is too large and the causes and effects are hard to trace and we need big data to be able to draw conclusions and small surveys are not as helpful as we might think.

There is an idea of looking for a doppelganger and it was first used in baseball. The goal was to predict the performance of good players as they age and if periods of low performance are temporary. Or would their performance diminish permanently, and they realized based on his performance in different ages that he’s going to bounce back from a low performance.

A powerful aspect of big data conducting AB testing has become extremely simple. We don’t need to pay people to do a survey or do anything for us. We just track their data and analyze their behavior based on data and then they record the whole Internet can be seen as a lab. This process helps us find casual effects easier.

There are thousands of AB testing being run at Google and Facebook. There’s also a company called Optimizely that specifically works on running AB tests for various third-party clients.

One of the studies is about if universities have an effect on how people's lives turn out. And they realize that the average salary for a Harwood graduate 10 years into their career is $50,000 more than an average of someone that attended Penn State university. This correlation doesn’t mean causation.

The curse of dimensionality: there’s so many variables and scenarios to consider at times during analysis we might come across the finding that is not necessarily accurate because of large amounts of variables available. Two examples would be the correlation found by scientists between IQ and a chemical compound between the brain and a hedge fund that tried to decide where the market is headed based on twitter sentiment analysis.

In this large amount of variables, if you just have enough that test, you will find (randomly) some correlation between variables that might not necessarily be accurate.

If we look for the number of searches for the Google stock logo, that would mean that there will be a high volume of transactions, but it wouldn’t predict if it would go up or down. If foreign investment in countries, such as Mexico or Brazil, increases based on Google searches, that will also fail because it’s rare that people would search about such things.

The book talks about how using Google search can help prevent crime, but it would be borderline privacy invasion. There are so many examples of how someone searched on how to kill someone and then they actually did it, but there are some downsides to this. For instance suicide ideation is common, but suicide is not: there far more suicide searches than actual suicide. So it doesn’t make sense for the police to show up for someone who searched for suicide.

But the searches could be used at a high level: for instance, whenever there is a high search for his Islam phobic content, hate crime probability rises. It would make sense for the police to patrol Muslim community and try to save moss or things

It would come peace by peace and we will have to stitch it together to be able to come up with reasonable conclusions
